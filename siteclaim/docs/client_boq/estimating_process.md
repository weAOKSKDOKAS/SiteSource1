# The Estimating Process

How a set of tender documents becomes a commercially viable tender submission.

## Definition

The estimating process is how we turn a set of tender documents into a commercially viable tender submission.

It is not just "calculating a number". It is about understanding scope, risk, cost, and terms well enough to sign a contract confidently.

---

## Outputs

Every estimate produces three outputs.

### 1. The Price

- The calculated contract value we submit to the client.
- Built from quantities, rates, assumptions, and risk allowances.

### 2. The Variability (Accuracy / Uncertainty)

- The expected accuracy of the estimate, often expressed as a ± range.
- Commonly referred to as the Class of Estimate:
  - Concept / Order of Magnitude
  - Feasibility
  - Budget
  - Tender / Definitive
- The class reflects:
  - Quality of information
  - Level of design completeness
  - Market uncertainty
  - Time pressure

Two prices can carry the same number but different variability.

### 3. The Terms & Conditions of the Offer

- What is included and excluded.
- What information we rely on.
- Who owns the quantities (client BOQ vs contractor take-off).
- Key assumptions.
- Qualifications and clarifications.

A price without terms is meaningless, because it carries no context.

---

## Inputs

Before pricing anything, the estimate is influenced by two categories of input.

### 2.1 Technical Inputs — Tender Specific

- Drawings
- Specifications
- Scope of Works
- BOQs (if provided)
- Contract conditions

### 2.2 Commercial & Strategic Inputs — Company Specific

- Market conditions and competition
- Client relationship
- Desire to win the project
- Risk appetite
- Available resources and workload

Two contractors pricing the same job will produce different prices because these inputs differ.

## The Estimating Process (Step-by-Step)

### Step 1: Tender & Scope Review

Goal: understand exactly what you are being asked to price.

- Start with the contract and Scope of Works.
- Identify:
  - Inclusions
  - Exclusions
  - Ambiguities
  - Conflicts between documents
- Review drawings and specs in detail.
- Generate:
  - Clarifying questions
  - Assumptions (where information is missing)

Output of Step 1:
- Clear definition of scope
- List of assumptions and clarifications
- Understanding of contractual risk

---

### Step 2: Estimate Structure & Pricing Schedule Setup

Goal: define how the scope will be priced and presented.

- Break the project scope into activities.
- Build a pricing schedule that:
  - Clearly describes what is included
  - Aligns with construction methodology
  - Aligns with procurement strategy

Example: "Labour to place concrete only – 20 m³" clearly excludes concrete supply.

Key principles — the pricing schedule is:
- A scope definition tool
- A risk management tool
- A communication tool

Direct vs Indirect Costs:
- Direct costs: shown to the client — labour, plant, materials, subcontract works.
- Indirect costs: usually not itemised in the schedule — risk allowances, weather allowances, supervision, temporary works, overheads.

Output of Step 2:
- Clear estimate structure
- Pricing schedule that defines scope
- Separation of direct and indirect costs

---

### Step 3: Cost Build-Up (Quantities × Rates)

Goal: work out what it will actually take to deliver the work.

For each item in the pricing schedule:

Decide the delivery method:
- Self-perform
- Subcontract
- Schedule of rates
- Hybrid approach

Break activities into resources:
- Quantities
- Labour
- Plant
- Materials
- Subcontract costs

Apply productivity & rates:
- Measure quantities
- Apply production rates
- Convert quantities into labour hours, crew sizes, and duration
- Apply cost rates

Example — Concrete Structure (self-perform):
- Formwork
  - m² of formwork
  - Labour hours + materials
- Reinforcement
  - Tons of steel
  - Subcontract rate per tonne
- Concrete
  - m³ of concrete
  - Concrete supply
  - Concreting crew
  - Pump (if required)

Repeat this process for every activity.

---

### Step 4: Indirect Costs & Allowances

Goal: capture costs not tied to individual activities.

Indirects are often driven by:
- Project duration (e.g. PM salaries)
- Contract value (e.g. risk allowances)

They are typically calculated after direct costs because directs drive duration and directs drive exposure.

Examples:
- Site management
- Temporary facilities
- Risk and contingency
- Weather allowances
- Corporate overhead recovery

Output of Step 4:
- Fully loaded project cost
- Understanding of cost drivers

---

### Step 5: Estimate Review & Validation

Goal: reduce the risk of errors and omissions.

Checks typically include:
- Scope coverage check
- Document compliance check
- Quantity sense checks
- Labour balance checks
- Rate benchmarking
- Risk review

This is where most money is either made or lost.

Output of Step 5:
- Confidence that the estimate is complete and defensible

---

### Step 6: Tender Submission & Offer Formation

Goal: clearly communicate your offer and protect yourself contractually.

Finalise:
- Price
- Pricing schedule
- Assumptions
- Clarifications

Prepare a Letter of Offer that:
- Qualifies scope
- States inclusions/exclusions
- References documents relied upon
- Links price to terms

This is the essential risk management step.

---

## Iterative

Estimating is an iterative process:

Tender Documents → Estimating → Addendum → Re-estimate → Review → Re-estimate → etc.

---

## The Basis for Commercial Negotiations

- The estimate becomes the baseline for negotiation.
- The submission becomes the basis of the contract.
- Any gaps here turn into:
  - Variations
  - Disputes
  - Margin erosion
